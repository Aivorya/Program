The trigger you think you have is not the trigger you actually have.

Ask most salespeople what throws them off and they say the same things. Rejection. Price pushback. A difficult prospect. These are categories. Categories are useless for training because you cannot interrupt a category — you can only interrupt a specific moment.

The silence after you give a number. Not silence in general — that specific silence, in that specific beat, when the number is out and nothing has come back yet. That is where some salespeople start explaining before they have been asked to. They fill the silence because the silence, to them, means no. The prospect has not said anything. The rejection has not happened. But the nervous system has already read the situation and issued a response — and the salesperson is already executing it.

That is the real trigger. Not rejection. The possibility of rejection, interpreted in a single moment.

The Trigger Catalogue you build today is not a reflection exercise. It is a diagnostic map. It documents the precise moments when your nervous system makes a decision before you do, and what that costs you in the conversation. This map gets referenced throughout the program. The more precisely it is built now, the more work it does later.

Precision is the point. A vague catalogue produces vague insight and no training value. The difference between "when they push back on price" and "when I give the number and they say nothing for three seconds, I assume no and start explaining before they have said anything" is the difference between a category and a mechanism. You can train against a mechanism. You cannot train against a category.

The Science

The ABC model, developed by Aaron Beck and refined by David Burns through cognitive behavioural therapy, describes how emotional reactions are generated. A is the activating event — the thing that happens. C is the consequence — the emotional and behavioural response. B is the automatic thought that connects them.

Most salespeople experience A and C and never examine B. The trigger fires, the reaction follows, and the sequence feels automatic because it is — but only because B has never been made visible.

B is the interpretation. It is the meaning the nervous system assigns to the event in the fraction of a second before the reaction generates. "Three seconds of silence" is A. "Explaining before being asked" is C. B — the automatic thought — is something like: silence means rejection is coming and I need to prevent it. That thought, operating invisibly, is what drives the behaviour.

Today's workbook makes B visible. That is all it does. Changing it comes later. Seeing it comes first.

The nervous system does not wait for evidence. It decides on possibility — and your behaviour follows before you have agreed to it.

Exercise 1 · Trigger Identification
From Category to Mechanism
5–6 minutes

Read the example below before you write anything.

Example — Vague vs Precise

Vague: "When they push back on price."

Precise: "When I give the number and they say nothing for more than three seconds — I assume it is a no and I start explaining before they have said anything."

The vague version names a category. The precise version names a moment, an interpretation, and a behaviour. Write to that level of precision.

List three triggers. For each one, complete all three columns.

Trigger — the exact moment
Automatic Reaction — what you do
Cost — what it costs you
Exercise 2 · Trigger Catalogue — Extended
The Ones That Take Longer to Name
7–8 minutes

Most salespeople can identify two or three surface triggers quickly. The ones that drive behaviour most reliably are the ones that take longer to name — usually because there is some embarrassment attached to them.

Complete the catalogue with a minimum of five additional entries. The catalogue will be used at Day 06, Day 12, Day 22, and Day 27. The more complete it is now, the more work it does later.

Consider: What happens when a colleague is mentioned as a comparison? What happens when a manager is listening? What happens when the deal is large enough to matter to your number? What happens when you are behind for the month?

These are the entries that matter most. Write them even if they are uncomfortable to name.

Exercise 3 · Reaction Replay
One Deal. One Moment.
6–7 minutes

Pick one lost deal. Not the most painful — the most instructive.

Do not walk through the whole deal. Find the exact moment regulation broke. Answer the three questions below about that moment only.

1. What was said or happened in the conversation immediately before you lost your footing?
2. What did you do in response — specifically, word for word if you can recall it?
3. What was the first moment you could have done something different — and what stopped you?

Write what actually happened. Not what you wish had happened. That record is the data. The data is what gets trained against.

End of Day — Day 02
Three Questions
3–4 minutes
1. Which trigger on your catalogue surprised you most — and what have you been telling yourself about that pattern that let it keep running?
2. In the Reaction Replay, at what point did you know the conversation had shifted — and did you name it at the time?
3. One thing from today that changes how you approach a call tomorrow.
Today's Practice Drill
Drill · Trigger Watch — Live Call

Before your next call today, pull one entry from your Trigger Catalogue — the one most likely to appear in the call you are about to have.

After the call, spend three minutes on this:

Write down the exact moment, if any, where that trigger fired. What was the reaction? What did it cost?

If the trigger did not fire, write that down too. A call where you held your footing is as useful as one where you did not — both are data.

No call today: take any conversation in the last 48 hours — a manager interaction, a negotiation, any moment of interpersonal pressure. Apply the same three minutes to that instead.
