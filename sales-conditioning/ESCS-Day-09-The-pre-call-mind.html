Phase 2 — Foundation
The Pre-Call Mind
What you have decided before you dial determines what you are able to hear once the conversation starts. Most salespeople do not regulate their state before a call. They regulate their script.
Morning Conditioning
From: Executive Conditioning System™ · Day 09 of 30 Read · Then Work

Before you dial, something has already happened.

You have formed a picture of how this conversation is going to go. It may feel like experience — a realistic read based on what you know about the account, the industry, or how the last three calls landed. It is not. It is a prediction. And a prediction is a closed interpretive frame.

A closed frame does not prevent you from hearing the buyer. It filters what you hear through what you have already decided. Questions that do not fit the prediction get processed differently. Silence gets interpreted through it. Hesitation confirms it. The conversation you thought you were entering turns out to be the conversation you brought.

This is not a character flaw. It is an automatic cognitive process — the brain conserving resources by pattern-matching new situations to previous ones. The problem is that pattern-matching runs faster than listening. By the time you ask the first question, the prediction is already doing the work.

The pre-call protocol you complete today has one purpose: interrupt that process before it runs.

The Science

In 2023, researchers at Stanford University ran a randomised controlled trial with 108 participants over 28 days, comparing three breathing techniques against mindfulness meditation. Primary outcomes were mood improvement and reduction in physiological arousal. Cyclic sighing won — two sharp inhales through the nose, one long slow exhale through the mouth. It outperformed box breathing, diaphragmatic breathing, and mindfulness meditation on both measures.

The mechanism is the extended exhale. Exhaling slowly activates the parasympathetic nervous system through the vagus nerve, reducing heart rate, lowering blood pressure, and measurably decreasing cortisol. The effect begins within two cycles.

What the study does not describe — but what performance psychology research makes clear — is that physiological arousal and cognitive interpretation are not separate systems. A nervous system running at elevated arousal interprets ambiguous stimuli as threatening. Silence on a call reads as rejection. Hesitation reads as objection. A nervous system running at baseline interprets the same stimuli as information.

The breath protocol does not just calm you down. It resets the cognitive frame from which you enter the conversation. That is a different mechanism, and a more useful one. Five cycles. Under 90 seconds. Run them before you dial — not after you feel activated. Before.

A salesperson who needs the call to go well has handed the buyer control of their nervous system before the conversation has started.

Workbook — Day 09
Exercise 1 · The Pre-Call Prediction Audit
What Are You Carrying In?
5 minutes. Do this before your next call today, or write it now for the next call on your calendar. Answer each question as it actually is — not the professional version.
1. What expectation am I carrying into this conversation?

Write what you actually think is going to happen — not what you hope.

2. Where did that expectation come from — this buyer, or something else?

Name it. Previous call with this person. A similar account that went badly. The last three rejections. This week's numbers. Something your manager said. Name the actual source.

3. What do I need this call to produce — and is that need affecting my state right now?

Not what you want. What you need. There is a difference, and you know it.

Exercise 2 · Wanting Versus Needing
The Release Practice
5 minutes. Read the section below, then complete the two prompts.

A salesperson who wants the deal to close brings full capability to the conversation. A salesperson who needs the deal to close has already handed the buyer partial control of their nervous system — and the buyer will detect it. Not as a conscious thought. As a low-level reading of your regulatory state, before a word of content is spoken.

Need produces approval-seeking orientation. Approval-seeking orientation activates the stress response under evaluative pressure. Elevated stress response degrades listening, curiosity, and composure. The buyer experiences a conversation partner who is subtly focused on their own outcome rather than on the buyer's situation. This is not a motivation problem. It is a physiological one.

Before your next call: name what you need the call to produce.

Set it aside for the duration of the call. Not because outcomes do not matter — because a salesperson focused on the quality of the conversation produces better outcomes than one focused on the outcome itself.

The distinction you are training: wanting the deal to close is useful motivation. Needing it to close — when the result feels necessary for your self-assessment, your standing, your security — transfers the locus of control to the buyer before you have asked a single question.

Where does that need come from today? Write one sentence.
Exercise 3 · Cyclic Sighing as Reset
The Interpretive Reset
90 seconds. Run this immediately after the audit and release above — before your next call.

Five cycles. Each cycle:

Two sharp inhales through the nose — the second one shorter and sharper than the first.
One long, slow exhale through the mouth — until the lungs are fully empty.

Do not rush the exhale. That is where the physiological effect lives.

After five cycles: sit with the result for 15 seconds. Notice the difference in how the upcoming call registers — not what you think about it, what it feels like to think about it.

This is not relaxation preparation. It is resetting the interpretive frame from which you will enter the conversation.
