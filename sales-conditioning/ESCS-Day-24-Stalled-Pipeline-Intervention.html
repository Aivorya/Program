Read · Then Work

You have deals that have gone quiet. Some have been silent for two weeks. Some longer.

You have not sent anything because there is nothing new to say — or because the last message went unanswered, and sending another one feels like confirming what you suspect: that the deal is dead and you did not want to know.

That is a normal response. It is also how live deals die.

A stalled deal is not a dead deal. It is a deal where a specific uncertainty was left unresolved when the conversation stopped — and the buyer's calculation of risk versus reward has temporarily settled on inaction. Not rejection. Inaction. The two are different situations requiring different responses.

What most salespeople do when a deal goes quiet is follow up. They send a message that says, in various forms: I still need an answer. Are you still there? I am waiting. They may not write those words, but that is the signal the message carries. The buyer reads the need before they read the content.

This is chasing. Chasing signals that you need a response more than the buyer needs to give one. That signal shifts the power of the conversation before a word of content is evaluated.

The alternative is not a different tone. It is a different structure.

A re-engagement message does not signal need. It offers something — a specific perspective, a piece of relevant information, an observation from a similar situation — that has value to the buyer independent of whether they ever purchase. When that is the signal the message carries, the buyer's calculation changes. Responding to the message does not mean recommitting to the deal. It means receiving something useful. That is a lower threshold, and a different emotional experience entirely.

The test is simple: would the buyer benefit from receiving this message even if they never buy? If yes, it is a re-engagement message. If no, it is chasing with different language. The distinction lives in the answer to that question — not in the tone, not in the opening, not in whether you use their first name.

Today you will audit every deal that has been silent for ten or more days, diagnose the specific uncertainty that produced the stall, and write one message per deal that addresses that uncertainty directly. Every message written today is sent or scheduled before the day ends. The audit without the output is analysis. The output is the work.

The Science

When a buyer goes quiet on a deal, the most common explanation salespeople assign is loss of interest. The more accurate explanation, documented in decision avoidance research (Anderson, 2003), is that the risk calculation has resolved in favour of inaction. A buyer who is uncertain about fit, timing, or implementation does not usually say no. No requires effort and social cost. Instead, they stop responding. The conversation that requires them to either recommit or explicitly decline becomes the conversation they avoid.

A follow-up message — "just checking in," "wanted to follow up," "are you still interested?" — makes this worse. Responding now requires the buyer to either recommit or explain why they have not. Both options carry cost. The message has raised the perceived cost of responding, which is the opposite of what the salesperson intended.

Reactance theory (Brehm, 1966) describes what happens when a person's autonomy feels pressured. The psychological response is to move away from the source of pressure, not toward it. A chase message signals need. The buyer's autonomy response is to withhold the response the salesperson is signalling they need.

A value-addition message does not trigger this. The buyer can read it, receive the value, and decide whether to respond without that decision being a recommitment to the deal. The perceived cost of responding drops. The conversation is easier to re-enter than to continue avoiding. The specificity of the message is the mechanism — a buyer who stalled on implementation uncertainty and receives a message that addresses implementation directly experiences something different from one who receives a generic check-in.

A stalled deal is a risk calculation resolved in favour of inaction. Your job is to change the calculation — not restate your interest in changing it.

Exercise 2 · Re-Engagement Message Construction
One targeted message per stalled deal

5–7 minutes. For each deal audited in Exercise 1, write one re-engagement message using the three-component structure below. Work through each component before writing the full message.

Component 1 — Specific reference
Something the buyer said or described in a previous conversation. Not "we spoke recently." A specific problem, concern, or detail they raised.
Component 2 — Specific value
A perspective, piece of information, or insight directly relevant to what they described. This is the reason for the message. It must be real — manufactured value is detectable and produces the same response as a chase message.
Component 3 — Low-friction ask
Something the buyer can say yes to with minimal commitment. Not "are you ready to move forward." Not "can we schedule a call." Something like: "worth ten minutes?" or "happy to send the detail if that would help?" or "would this be useful to share with your team?"

The distinction in practice
Chase message
"Hi [name], just wanted to follow up on our conversation from a few weeks ago. Are you still looking at this? Happy to answer any questions."
Re-engagement message
"Hi [name] — I have been thinking about what you mentioned regarding the reporting lag in your Q3 reviews. We worked with a similar situation at a company of your size and the approach was different from what I described. Worth ten minutes if you are open to it?"
The chase message references nothing, offers nothing, and implicitly asks for recommitment. The re-engagement message references something specific, offers something specific, and asks for something the buyer can say yes to without deciding anything commercial. The buyer's nervous system reads the difference before conscious evaluation begins.
