Phase 1 — Diagnosis
Identity Audit
Behaviour is downstream of identity. Before you can change what you do, you need to see clearly what you currently believe about yourself.
Morning Conditioning
From: Executive Conditioning System™ · Day 03 of 30 Read · Then Work

Behaviour is downstream of identity. Not aspiration — identity. The story you are currently telling yourself about what kind of salesperson you are is not background noise. It is the operating system your nervous system runs on when pressure arrives.

Here is the mechanism: the self-concept functions as a performance regulator. It establishes a set point — a level of performance the nervous system treats as normal, sustainable, and safe. When performance approaches that set point from below, activation increases. When performance begins to exceed it consistently, a different kind of activation occurs. Not effort. Correction. The system pulls back toward what it has decided is normal for you.

This is not a psychological concept. It is a behavioural one. The salesperson who closes 10 deals in a month, then 10 again, then 10 again — and stalls there — is not hitting a skill ceiling. The skill is present. They are hitting the ceiling of what the self-concept has established as available. Until that ceiling is identified and contested, no amount of technique will reliably move it.

Phase 1 has been diagnostic. Day 01 installed the observer. Day 02 named the triggers — the precise moments when the nervous system fires before you do. Today goes one level deeper. Underneath the trigger is an automatic thought. Underneath the automatic thought is a belief. And underneath the belief, consistently, is a self-concept statement: I am the kind of salesperson who...

Today, you write that statement ten times. Then you sort what you wrote.

What you are looking for is not your weaknesses in the motivational sense. You are looking for the beliefs you have been treating as facts — the ones that are running your behaviour without your permission.

The Science

Self-concept theory, formalised in performance contexts through Albert Bandura's work on self-efficacy, holds that an individual's working model of themselves functions as a behavioural regulator — not just a set of beliefs about character or worth.

The research is consistent across decades: people perform in ways that confirm their self-concept more reliably than in ways that contradict it. The correction behaviour shows up before the salesperson is aware of it — over-explaining at the close, conceding on price before being pushed, losing composure precisely when the deal is closest to landing. This is not weakness. It is the system confirming what it already believes about what is available.

James Clear's synthesis of this research adds a practical layer: behaviour change that targets identity — who am I, rather than what do I want — is more durable than behaviour change that targets outcomes. This is why the program ends with an identity declaration built from evidence rather than aspiration. Today is where you map what the current identity actually says.

The comparison dynamic connects through Deci and Ryan's Self-Determination Theory. Approval-oriented motivation — performing to feel competent relative to others — is measurably less stable under evaluative pressure than values-oriented motivation. When you look at a colleague's numbers and feel something shift in you, that shift is identity data. And it costs you something specific in the calls that follow.

Workbook — Day 03
Exercise 1 · Identity Statement Audit
The Current Self-Concept
5–7 minutes
Complete the following sentence ten times. Write quickly. Do not edit as you go — write the first completion that comes to mind each time.
I am the kind of salesperson who...
Now sort what you wrote into three categories.
Category A — True and useful. Accurate self-assessments that serve your performance. These are the foundation.
Category B — True but limiting. Accurate, but they function as ceilings rather than floors. Note them. These are what the program targets from Day 13 onward.
Category C — Not true. A story being treated as a fact. These have never been tested against actual evidence. They feel true because they have been rehearsed — not because they have been proven.
Write which of your ten statements land in each category.

The Category C statements are today's primary target.

Exercise 2 · The Downward Arrow
From Surface Pattern to Core Belief
5–7 minutes
Take one statement from Category C or Category B. Write it at the top. Then ask: If that were true — if that really were what kind of salesperson I am — what would it mean about me? Write the answer. Ask the same question of that answer. Repeat three to four times. Stop when you reach something short and blunt.
Starting statement:
If that were true, what would it mean about me as a salesperson?
And if that were true, what would it mean about me?
And if that were true, what would it mean about me?
Core belief (the final, blunt statement):
Example of the movement: I am the kind of salesperson who loses credibility when the price comes up. ↓ What would that mean about me? That I cannot handle the part of the conversation that matters most. ↓ What would that mean about me? That I do not actually believe in the value of what I am selling. ↓ What would that mean about me? That I am not competent enough to deserve the close.
On a scale of 0–100, how convinced are you that this core belief is true right now?
Conviction rating:
/ 100

This number is your baseline. It will be used again at Day 22 and Day 30.

Exercise 3 · Comparison Inventory
The Cost of Comparison
5–7 minutes
Name one specific person on your team or in your network you compare yourself to. Use initials or a single word — not a name you would share publicly.
Who:
When you see their numbers, their name on a leaderboard, or their activity in a shared system — what happens in you? Describe it physically first, then mentally.
Name precisely what happens to your next call after that comparison. Not philosophically — behaviourally. What specifically shifts?
What does that shift cost you, in performance terms, on that call?

This is not an exercise in eliminating comparison. It is an exercise in knowing what it does to you — so you can account for it rather than pretend it does not happen.

The ceiling is almost always an identity ceiling first.

Today's Practice Drill
Pre-Call Conditioning Drill · Day 03

No live call required for this drill. This is a pre-call conditioning exercise.

Before your next sales call, reread the core belief you reached at the bottom of the Downward Arrow.

Then ask yourself: On this specific call, with this specific buyer — is that belief relevant?

You do not need to resolve it today. You need to catch it operating — before it has already decided how the call goes.

90 seconds · Before the call starts

If you have no calls today, apply this before any high-stakes interaction — a manager conversation, a pipeline review, a proposal delivery. The conditioning is the same.
