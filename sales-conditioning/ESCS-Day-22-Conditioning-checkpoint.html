Morning Conditioning
From: Executive Conditioning System™ · Day 22 of 30 Read · Then Work

Roger Bannister did not simply train harder than the runners who came before him.

He trained with weekly targets broken into specific components — stride efficiency, pacing intervals, oxygen recovery time. He measured each one separately. He knew, at each stage of the training cycle, the precise gap between where he currently was and where the performance required him to be. The measurement was not administrative. It was part of the method. Without it, the training was general. With it, the training compounded.

That is the structure of today.

Phase 3 gave you ten days of deliberate pressure — objection rehearsal, AI roleplay, contrast training, dissonance work. What that pressure actually changed is not a feeling. It is a set of behavioral observations that either exist or they do not. The scorecard below is the instrument that reads them.

Rate what the evidence shows. Not what you hope is true. Not what it feels like has shifted. What you can point to in a specific conversation from the past ten days. That standard has not changed since Day 12.

Where the scores have moved: name the behavior that moved them. This is the first draft of the evidence your Day 30 Identity Declaration will use. Write it precisely.

Where the scores have not moved: this is Phase 4 targeting information. It is not a failure report. It is the specific address for the next eight days of live-deal work. A participant who identifies two precise dimensions to consolidate in Phase 4 has a fundamentally different program from Day 23 onward than one who treats all eight dimensions equally. The targeting is the training.

The Science

Black and Wiliam's 1998 review of classroom learning research — Inside the Black Box — is one of the most replicated findings in learning science. The core result: formative assessment, ongoing measurement that informs what to practice next, produces significantly better learning outcomes than practice without it. The participant who knows specifically what has and has not changed can target subsequent practice. The one who does not is improving everything approximately — which means improving nothing specifically.

This connects to Ericsson's deliberate practice principle. Deliberate practice is not repetition — it is repetition at the edge of current ability, with a specific target. The Day 22 scorecard produces the targeting that makes Phase 4 deliberate rather than general.

There is a third element: metacognition — the accuracy of your self-assessment. A participant who has now completed three formal measurement points (Day 01, Day 12, Day 22) has a more accurate picture of their actual performance than one who has been practicing without it. That accuracy changes what you work on and how you calibrate risk in live calls.

Exercise 3 · Phase 4 Targeting
Two or three dimensions. Specific format. No more.
5–7 minutes

Select two — or at most three — dimensions where the delta is zero or where the behavioral evidence is weak. These are your Phase 4 targets.

For each, write a targeting statement in the when-then format. Not a general intention. A specific behavioral description of when the pattern breaks and what the target behavior is.

Format: "When [specific trigger in a live call], my [dimension] breaks at [the specific moment]. Phase 4 target: [specific behavioral instruction] in [specific call context]."

Example: "When a buyer raises price before I have established the cost of inaction, my regulation breaks at the moment I state the number. Phase 4 target: hold five seconds of silence after every price statement, regardless of internal pressure."

The discipline here is selecting two or three — not all eight. A focused Phase 4 produces compounding improvement. A general one produces general progress.
