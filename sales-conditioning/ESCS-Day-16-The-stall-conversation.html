Phase 3 — Conditioning
The Stall Conversation
"We need to think about it" is not a decision. It is an avoidance of a decision. The salesperson who accepts it as an answer has accepted a conversation that will never close. The one who understands what produces it can have a different conversation entirely.
Morning Conditioning
From: Executive Conditioning System™ · Day 16 of 30
Read · Then Work

When a buyer says "we need to think about it," they are not communicating ambivalence about your solution. They are communicating that the perceived cost of committing now feels higher than the perceived benefit of the best available option. That calculation may be wrong. But it is the calculation they have made, and no amount of urgency from your side changes it — it reinforces it.

Christopher Anderson's 2003 research on decision avoidance documents what most salespeople already know but rarely name correctly: people choose inaction not because they have decided against an option, but because inaction feels safer than a decision that might be wrong. The stall is not a soft no. It is a risk calculation that has temporarily resolved in favour of doing nothing.

The practical implication: the stall is diagnostic information. It is telling you that some specific risk is present — a risk that has not been addressed, possibly one that has not been named. Your job is to find out which one. You cannot do that by responding to the stall as if it were the final answer.

What you also cannot do is push. Jack Brehm's reactance research, running from 1966 through decades of replication, establishes what happens when freedom of choice is perceived as threatened: resistance increases. Not stays the same — increases. A buyer who was genuinely considering saying yes becomes more committed to non-decision when pressure is applied. Urgency tactics, artificial deadlines, and commitment questions aimed at forcing movement do not accelerate the sale. They contract it. The stall becomes harder to exit because the buyer is now defending their position rather than examining it.

The stall diagnosis protocol works for the opposite reason. It does not push toward a decision. It asks what the buyer needs in order to make one. That distinction is not a courtesy. It is the mechanism that keeps the conversation open.

The Science

Two mechanisms explain why stalls happen and why the standard response makes them worse.

Anderson (2003) on decision avoidance: people choose inaction when the perceived cost of deciding outweighs the perceived benefit of the best available option. In a buying context, this is rarely about the product. It is about what happens if the decision is wrong — internally, financially, reputationally. The stall is not evidence that the solution is unappealing. It is evidence that some cost of commitment has not been resolved.

Brehm (1966) on psychological reactance: when a person perceives their freedom to choose as threatened, they experience a motivational state directed at restoring that freedom. In practice, pressure toward a decision produces resistance to that decision. The buyer becomes more committed to not deciding, not less. The stall diagnosis protocol avoids this entirely by framing the next move around what the buyer needs, not what you need.

The stall is not a decision. It is unresolved risk — still in the conversation, still addressable.

Exercise 1 · Stall Diagnosis Protocol
The Diagnostic Question
Approx. 7 minutes. Read the protocol. Write your version. Map the responses.
The following question cannot be answered without the buyer revealing which specific concern is present:
"Of course — what would be most helpful to think through? Is it the fit, the timing, the investment, or something else?"
The four options map directly to the uncertainty types from Day 10. Fit concerns are outcome uncertainty. Timing concerns are usually implementation or social uncertainty. Investment concerns return to the price conversation from Day 15. "Something else" is an open door — stay with it.
Two working variations:
"What's the part that would be most useful to have clearer before you move forward?"
"Is there something specific I haven't addressed that would help you decide?"
Write your version below. Same function, your language. One sentence. It must end with an open question.
Response Mapping
Each answer the buyer gives requires a different next move. Do not attempt to run the same response to every stall type.
Fit
Outcome uncertainty. Return to the specific problem and the specific evidence that this addresses it. Do not re-present the solution. Re-anchor to the problem.
Timing
Surface the constraint. Is it a budget cycle, an internal approval process, or is timing a proxy for something else? "What's driving the timing for you?" One question.
Investment
Return to the re-anchoring sequence from Day 15. This is a price conversation. Treat it as one.
Something else
Ask what it is. Do not suggest options. One question: "What would it be?" Then hold the silence. The buyer who says "something else" has more to say.

Exercise 2 · Next Step Commitment
Closing the Loop
Approx. 5 minutes. A conversation that ends without a specific agreed next step has produced nothing actionable.
Peter Gollwitzer's research on implementation intentions is consistent and replicated: specific if-then plans — when X, I will do Y — produce dramatically higher follow-through than general intentions. "I'll follow up" is not a next step. It is an intention with no structure. A vague close leaves both parties with no committed action. A specific close gives both parties a structure to return to.
"What would make sense as a next step — would it be useful to [specific action] by [specific date]?"
This question preserves the buyer's autonomy by offering a concrete option rather than a vague obligation. It also commits both parties to something real.
Write three versions for your three most common conversation-close scenarios. Be specific about the action and the timeframe.

Day 16 Drill — Stall Diagnosis · Pre-Call and Post-Call

Before your next call: identify the most likely stall point in this specific conversation. Write one sentence on which uncertainty type — if unresolved — would produce it.

After the call: if a stall occurred, write exactly what was said and which diagnosis question you used or should have used. If no stall occurred, write the next step commitment you closed with — and whether it was specific enough to hold.
