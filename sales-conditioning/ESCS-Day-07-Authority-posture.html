Phase 2 — Foundation
The Authority Posture
Posture and voice are not style choices. They are physiological signals that change both your internal state and how the person across from you reads the conversation.
Morning Conditioning
From: Executive Conditioning System™ · Day 07 of 30 Read · Then Work

The relationship between body and mind runs in both directions.

You already know that mental state affects posture — anxiety produces collapse, urgency produces tension, confidence produces openness. What receives less attention is the reverse: that deliberate posture change produces measurable changes in internal state, and that the buyer reads your physical signals before they process a word you say.

This is not a style point. It is how the nervous system operates.

Upright, open posture signals safety to your own nervous system, which keeps your prefrontal cortex online — the part responsible for language, listening, and non-reactive decision-making. Collapsed or contracted posture signals threat, which begins routing cognitive resources away from exactly the systems you need in a sales conversation. The buyer's nervous system is running the same assessment on you. Before any evaluation of your proposition begins, their threat-detection system has already read your physical state and made a preliminary judgment about whether this conversation is safe to engage with.

A regulated physical presence creates the conditions for social engagement. An unregulated one — tense, collapsed, or performing composure — creates low-level friction before the conversation has started.

In 2010, Amy Cuddy and colleagues published research on expansive posture and performance in evaluative situations. The finding that attracted the most attention — hormonal change — has not replicated cleanly in subsequent studies. What has replicated: participants who adopted open, expansive posture before high-pressure evaluative tasks reported higher confidence and performed better on those tasks than those who did not. The subjective confidence effect is real. The specific hormonal mechanism remains debated. The practical application stands regardless of the mechanism.

The participant who stands on calls, holds open posture, and speaks at a deliberate pace is not performing confidence. They are creating the physiological conditions for it. That distinction matters because performance collapses under pressure. Physiology is more durable.

The same mechanism operates in your voice before a word of content is evaluated.

Speech rate, pause length, and vocal tone are processed as authority signals before the content of what you say is evaluated. A slower speech rate with deliberate pauses signals control. A faster rate with filled pauses — "um," "uh," "does that make sense?" — signals anxiety and approval-seeking. Listeners do not consciously analyze this. The assessment happens automatically, and shapes how everything that follows is received.

Three components operate here. Rate: under pressure, most people speak faster than their baseline. Record a call segment and listen for where your rate increases — it clusters around price, timeline, and direct questions. Pause: the deliberate pause after a question or proposal signals that you are not anxious about the response. Filling it signals the opposite, and the buyer reads it accurately. Approval language: "Does that make sense?" "If that's okay." "Just quickly." These are not neutral phrases. Each one signals that you need the conversation to go well. Identify the specific phrases you use. They cluster around the same moments your trigger catalogue named in Phase 1.

The Science — Embodied Cognition

The field of embodied cognition documents that the body and mind are not a one-way system. Mental states produce physical responses — this is well established. What is less intuitive is that physical states produce mental responses with equal reliability.

In studies on embodied cognition, participants assigned to physically enact emotional states — expansive versus contracted posture, upright versus slumped position — showed measurable differences in cognitive performance, risk tolerance, and self-reported confidence on subsequent tasks. The body does not simply express the mind. It participates in generating it.

The practical implication: deliberately changing your physical state before a call is not cosmetic preparation. It is a physiological intervention that changes the state you bring into the conversation. The nervous system does not distinguish between genuine and rehearsed — it responds to the physical signal it receives.

The buyer's nervous system reads your physical state before their conscious mind evaluates your proposition.

Exercise 2 · Voice Audit
Locating the Approval Patterns

Pull up a recording of a recent call — or make a short practice recording now. Two minutes is enough. Listen specifically for the three components.

Rate

Filled Pauses

How often do you use "um," "uh," or trailing phrases? Note the approximate frequency and which moments trigger them.

Approval Language

Exercise 3 · Silence Tolerance Drill
Five Seconds, Timed
10 minutes — Calibrate five seconds of silence so it is available in a live call when you need it. The discomfort you feel here is the same discomfort that fills silences in real conversations.

Solo practice — do this now.

Set a timer or use a clock. Ask the following question out loud to an empty room:

"What's the main thing you'd want to be different about how this is working now?"

After you ask it — stop. Time five seconds. Do not speak. Hold it.

Repeat with:

"What would need to be true for this to be worth prioritising right now?"

Five seconds. Timed. Hold it.

Note what happens in your body during the five seconds:

That physical response is what you are training to tolerate. The drill is not about silence as a tactic. It is about extending your threshold for discomfort so that you do not break it automatically when it costs you.

On calls today: Ask a question, then hold five seconds before speaking again. Not "as long as feels right." Five seconds. If the buyer has not responded within five seconds, you may prompt — once, briefly. Track how many times you deploy it and whether you held the full count.
