AI Roleplay — Resistant Buyer
Simulation is not performance. But simulation with a structured debrief is the closest available approximation to deliberate practice under pressure. Read · Then Work

The roleplay is not the work.

Most people will run the simulation and feel like they have done something. They will feel the pressure of a resistant buyer, recover from a price challenge, navigate a stall. They will close the window, note it went reasonably well or badly, and move on.

That is repetition. It is not deliberate practice, and it will not produce different results next week.

The debrief is where the skill is built. Not during the simulation — after it. Ericsson's thirty years of research across music, surgery, chess, and sport reached the same conclusion each time: structured reflection immediately after performance is what converts repetition into development. Without it, you entrench whatever pattern you ran — including the bad ones. The simulation without the debrief is just a faster way to rehearse your existing habits.

Today has three rounds. Each round is ten minutes. After each round, you stop. You write the four debrief questions. You do not skip this step. You do not abbreviate it. The debrief takes three to five minutes. That is where the work is.

The buyer you are about to simulate will respond differently depending on how you operate. When you are genuinely curious — asking because you want to know, not because you know you are supposed to — the buyer opens. When you become defensive or start managing toward a decision, the buyer escalates resistance. This is not a quirk of the simulation. It is the simulation telling you something true. Pay attention to when the buyer shifts. That shift is data.

Three rounds. Three debriefs. Then a cross-round comparison. Then four dimensions from the Foundation Scorecard. Total: approximately 45 minutes.

Do not run round two until you have written the round one debrief. Do not run round three until you have written the round two debrief. The sequencing is not optional.

The Science

Simulation fidelity and transfer is a measurable relationship. Research from surgical training, aviation, and military contexts is consistent: the closer a simulation matches the conditions of real performance — including the emotional activation — the higher the transfer to live performance. A resistant buyer who escalates pressure when met with defensive answers is a higher-fidelity simulation than a cooperative buyer who makes the conversation easy. The discomfort of the simulation is not a side effect. It is the mechanism.

The debrief works because post-performance reflection, when structured and immediate, produces more accurate self-assessment than unstructured review. Asking "what triggered you at the specific moment when the buyer raised price?" produces a usable answer. Asking "how did it go?" does not. The specificity of the question determines the specificity of the answer — and the specificity of the answer determines whether the next round is different.

One more thing the simulation tests: whether the conversation structure from Day 11 has been sufficiently internalised. If it has, working memory is available for regulation, curiosity, and uncertainty diagnosis. If it has not, you will feel the cognitive cost — managing structure while simultaneously managing the conversation. The debrief question on structure is not rhetorical. It surfaces exactly this.

The simulation is the stimulus. The debrief is the learning. One without the other is not deliberate practice — it is repetition with extra steps.

Exercise 1 · The AI Prompt
Deploy Before Round 1
Copy the prompt below and paste it directly into ChatGPT or Claude. Do not modify it. The prompt is calibrated to produce a buyer who responds to your inputs — not a scripted scenario. The buyer's behaviour will change depending on how you operate. Approximately 2 minutes setup.
"You are playing the role of a B2B buyer named Alex. You are a Head of Operations at a mid-sized company. You have agreed to this call but are skeptical — you are currently using a competitor solution you are reasonably satisfied with. You are price-sensitive and will raise cost as an objection. You are not hostile, but you are direct and not easily impressed. When the salesperson answers your concerns with genuine curiosity and specific questions, you become slightly more open. When they become defensive or try to push you toward a decision, you become more resistant. Respond naturally to what they say. Do not follow a script. Begin the call by saying: 'I'll be honest — we're pretty happy with what we have. I'm not sure this call is going to change that, but I said I'd give you fifteen minutes. What have you got?'"
If AI roleplay is not available: brief a colleague on the buyer profile above and run the session live. Alternatively, narrate both sides aloud into a recording device — play both roles with full commitment. The debrief protocol is identical regardless of format.
Exercise 2 · Round Debrief Protocol
Four Questions — After Every Round
Run this immediately after Round 1. Then again after Round 2. Then again after Round 3. Same questions each time. The repetition is deliberate — you are tracking whether your answers change. Do not begin the next round until you have completed this debrief. 3–5 minutes per debrief.
Round 1 Debrief
Q1 — What was the specific moment in this round that triggered a defensive or reactive response in you? Name the moment — not the general feeling.
Q2 — When were you genuinely curious — asking because you wanted to know — versus performing curiosity as a technique? Name the specific exchange.
Q3 — Which phase of the conversation structure did you lose or rush? What were you managing at that moment that competed with structure?
Q4 — In the next round, at the moment you identified in Q1 — what specifically will you do differently?
