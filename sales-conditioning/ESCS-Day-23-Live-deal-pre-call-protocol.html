Read · Then Work

You are twenty-two days into a conditioning protocol. The drills from Phase 3 that felt artificial — the silence holds, the objection pauses, the breath before you respond — are what should be showing up automatically in your live calls now. Phase 4 is where you find out whether they are.

If they are showing up: the debrief confirms it and sharpens it.
If they are not: the debrief surfaces exactly where the transfer has not yet happened. That is not a setback. That is a targeting instruction for the remaining calls.

Ericsson spent thirty years studying expert performance across surgery, music, chess, and sport. The consistent finding: experience without structured reflection produces one year of learning repeated however many times you show up. The mechanism for converting experience into deliberate practice is not effort or intention. It is the feedback loop — the immediate, written, structured reflection that follows the performance. Without it, the calls of Phase 4 accumulate. With it, they compound.

You ran this loop on Day 17 for the AI roleplay debrief. Same mechanism. Same requirement. It applies with identical force to live calls — in fact with greater force, because live calls are higher-fidelity than simulations. The stakes are real. The buyer's responses are unscripted. The pressure is genuine. That makes the debrief more valuable, not less necessary.

Today installs two things. Neither is new. Both are sharper.

The first is a targeting layer added to the pre-call protocol you have been running since Day 09. One step. Fifteen seconds. It connects the call you are about to have to the specific behavioral dimensions from the Day 22 scorecard that have not yet moved. This converts the call from a general practice session into a targeted one.

The second is the post-call debrief protocol. Three questions. Five minutes. Written, not thought. Every significant call this week, without exception.

The call is the stimulus. The debrief is where the learning happens.

The Science

Ericsson's research distinguishes between naive practice and deliberate practice. Naive practice is repetition in the domain — accumulating calls, accumulating experience, assuming that volume produces improvement. Deliberate practice is structured repetition at the edge of current ability, with a specific behavioral target and immediate feedback on whether the target was achieved.

The distinction is not about effort. A salesperson running thirty calls a week can be engaged, focused, and working hard — and still be running naive practice if there is no specific behavioral target set before the call and no structured reflection after it.

The post-call debrief is the feedback mechanism that makes the difference. Research in surgical training and elite sport shows the same pattern: structured immediate reflection after performance produces more accurate skill self-perception and more specific behavioral targets than unstructured experience, regardless of how many repetitions have accumulated. The structure matters. The immediacy matters. The five-minute constraint matters — it keeps the debrief operational rather than reflective.

Twenty calls without a debrief is twenty calls of experience. Twenty calls with one is twenty deliberate practice sessions. The protocol is what makes the difference.

Workbook — Day 23
Exercise 1 · Pre-Call Protocol — Targeting Layer
Adding the Targeting Step
5 minutes · Before your first call today

The pre-call protocol from Day 09 is unchanged. Add one step before you begin it.

Pull out the Phase 4 targeting statements from Day 22. Read them. Identify which one is most relevant to the specific call you are about to have — the buyer, the stage, the likely pressure points. Write it here as a behavioral objective. One sentence. Specific enough that you can evaluate it after the call.

I will be more curious.

I will try to stay regulated.

When the buyer pushes back on price, I will ask one question before I respond.

When there is silence after I present the proposal, I will hold it for a full count of five.

The objective must be evaluable. If you cannot score it yes or no at the end of the call, it is not specific enough. This step takes fifteen seconds. It converts the call from general to targeted.

Exercise 2 · Post-Call Debrief Protocol
Three Questions — Every Significant Call
5 minutes · Written · Immediately after the call

Three questions. Answer them immediately after the call — not at end of day, not in your head. The accuracy of self-assessment degrades with time and distance. Write it now.

Question 1 — Where did I stay regulated?
Name one specific moment. What did regulation look like — what did you do?
Question 2 — Where did I slip?
Name one specific moment. What was the trigger? What did you do instead of what you had targeted?
Question 3 — What is the one specific behavioral change for the next call?
Write it in when-then format: When [specific trigger], I will [specific behavior].

The participant who shortens this to a mental note is managing the discomfort of honest self-assessment rather than using it. That is a choice. Make it consciously if you make it.
