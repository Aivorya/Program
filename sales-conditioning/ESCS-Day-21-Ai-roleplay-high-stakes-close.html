Phase 3 — Conditioning
AI Roleplay — High-Stakes Close
The resistant buyer on Day 17 tested regulation under pressure. Today's simulation tests something harder: staying regulated when the deal is close, the buyer is engaged, and the pressure to close comes from inside, not from the buyer.
Morning Conditioning
From: Executive Conditioning System™ · Day 21 of 30 Read · Then Work

On Day 17, the simulation worked because the buyer was difficult. Resistance is a familiar pressure profile. You train for it. You feel it arrive and you know what to do with it.

Today's simulation is harder. Not because the buyer will push back — they won't, not much. Because the buyer is engaged, the deal is close, and the pressure is coming from inside.

This is the activation pattern that costs more deals than any difficult buyer does. When success is possible — when the buyer has budget, authority, and genuine interest — the nervous system registers proximity to a desired outcome. That proximity activates approach-avoidance arousal: wanting shifts toward needing, and needing degrades execution in ways that are invisible until the conversation is already off.

Research in performance psychology is consistent on this: performers don't choke under certain failure. They choke under achievable success. The salesperson who stayed regulated through five Guaranteed No repetitions on Day 13 may find a genuinely interested buyer harder to handle than a hostile one. If that sounds counterintuitive, it's because we assume difficulty is the variable. It isn't. Proximity to the desired outcome is the variable.

Today's simulation is designed to test that specific pressure profile. The buyer is close to yes. The close is possible. That's the pressure.

Run the Day 09 pre-call protocol before you open the AI. The wanting-versus-needing audit in Step 1 applies directly here: do you need this simulation to go well, or do you want it to go well? Name it honestly. The simulation that begins from a needing state produces data about what happens under approval-seeking pressure. The simulation that begins from a wanting state produces data about what you can actually do. Both are valid. They are not the same session.

The Science

In performance psychology, choking under pressure refers specifically to underperformance in high-stakes conditions relative to practice conditions. The mechanism is not anxiety — it's attentional shift. Under high-stakes achievable conditions, performers move from process focus (what am I doing right now) to outcome focus (how is this going), which overloads working memory and disrupts automated sequences.

The critical finding: this is most activated not by difficult conditions but by achievable ones. The proximity of a desired outcome generates approach-avoidance conflict — wanting it strongly while fearing the loss of it — which produces elevated arousal that execution cannot absorb cleanly.

In sales, this is most common in the final meeting with an engaged buyer. The skill is present. The regulation isn't. The Day 09 wanting-versus-needing distinction is the practical intervention: not to remove the wanting, but to release the needing that converts the outcome into a threat.

The hardest regulation challenge is not the buyer who won't move. It is the buyer who might.

Pre-Simulation — Day 09 Protocol
Before You Begin
Run the Full Pre-Call Protocol
Approximate time: 3 minutes

Before opening the AI, complete each step in order.

Step 1 — Wanting versus needing audit

Write one sentence: do you need this simulation to go well, or do you want it to go well? If the honest answer is need, name what the need is attached to. It is probably not about the simulation.

Step 2 — Cyclic sighing

Five cycles. Two sharp inhales through the nose, one long exhale through the mouth. Full exhale each time. Do not skip this.

Step 3 — Process focus statement

Write one sentence: what specific behaviour are you focusing on in this simulation? Not the outcome — the behaviour. One thing.

Workbook — Day 21
Exercise 1 · The Simulation
High-Stakes Final Meeting — Full Deal Arc
Approximate time: 30–45 minutes including debrief rounds
The buyer profile — use this prompt exactly
"You are playing the role of a B2B buyer named Sarah. You are a Chief Operating Officer at a company of 200 people. You have been through two discovery calls and have seen the proposal. You are genuinely interested — this solution addresses a real problem your business has. However, you have one unresolved concern: you are not confident that your team will actually adopt the solution, and you have seen previous initiatives fail at the implementation stage. You are not resistant — you want this to work. But you will not commit until you feel certain about the implementation risk. When the salesperson asks genuine questions about your concern and addresses it with specific evidence, you become more open. When they push toward a close before your concern is fully addressed, you pull back. Begin the conversation by saying: 'Thanks for making time. I've been thinking about this since we last spoke. I'm close — but I have one thing I need to work through before I can commit.'"
The simulation structure — run the full meeting arc in this order
  • Phase 1 — Confirm understanding. Open with a summary of what was diagnosed across the two discovery calls. Check for accuracy. Correct anything Sarah adjusts.
  • Phase 2 — Proposal. Present the solution as a direct response to what was diagnosed. Not a feature list. A response to a specific problem.
  • Phase 3 — The concern surfaces. Apply the objection anatomy from Day 14. What is the surface concern? What is the uncertainty underneath it?
  • Phase 4 — Uncertainty diagnosis. Identify the category of uncertainty (Day 10). Sarah is carrying social uncertainty — the cost of being wrong internally, the failure of previous initiatives, the risk of sponsoring something that doesn't stick. Address that specifically, not the general hesitation.
  • Phase 5 — Certainty building. Use the certainty language from Day 10. Address the specific uncertainty Sarah named — implementation risk and internal adoption. Not the product. Not the price. The implementation risk.
  • Phase 6 — Collaborative close. Use the language from Day 18. Do not close until the concern is addressed. If you close before the concern is resolved, Sarah pulls back.
  • Phase 7 — If the close does not land. Apply the stall diagnosis from Day 16. Identify what is unresolved. Commit to a specific next step — date, agenda, owner.

Run minimum two rounds. Three if time allows. Debrief after each round using Exercise 2 before running the next round.

Exercise 2 · Debrief Protocol
Four Questions — After Every Round
Approximate time: 5 minutes per round

Complete after each round before running the next. Do not combine rounds in a single debrief.

Q1 — Regulation

At what moment did you feel the most activation? What was the physical signal? What did you do with it?

Q2 — Curiosity vs defence

When Sarah's concern surfaced — did you ask a question or did you start building a case? Write what you actually did, not what you intended to do.

Q3 — The price moment

At the price moment — did you state the number and hold silence, or did you add qualifications? What state were you in when you gave the price?

Q4 — Observation vs judgement

Write one observation about the round. Not an evaluation — a description of what happened. Specific. Present tense. No interpretation.

Exercise 3 · Cross-Round Comparison
After All Rounds
Approximate time: 5 minutes

Complete after all rounds are finished.

Q1 — Regulation trajectory

Did your regulation improve across rounds, hold steady, or deteriorate? Describe specifically what changed.

Q2 — The close

In which round did you hold the collaborative close most cleanly? What was different in that round?

Q3 — Day 17 comparison

Compare your regulation in this simulation to Day 17. Where were you more regulated? Where were you less? What does the difference tell you about which pressure profile is harder for you?

Today's Practice Drill
Day 21 Drill · Pre-Call Protocol — Live Application

The simulation is complete. Before your next real call today or tomorrow, run the full Day 09 pre-call protocol — wanting versus needing audit, five cycles of cyclic sighing, one process focus statement.

If you have a high-stakes call in the next 48 hours — a buyer who is engaged and close to deciding — that is the call to run it on. That is the pressure profile you trained for today.

If no call is scheduled, identify the one deal in your pipeline where you feel the wanting-to-needing pull most strongly. Write the deal name and one sentence on what the needing is attached to.
