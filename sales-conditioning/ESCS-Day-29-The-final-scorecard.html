Read · Then Work

There is a specific reason why two salespeople can go through identical training, produce similar results during the program, and diverge significantly six months later. One attributes what changed to the program — or to a good patch of deals, or to a manager who gave them space, or to timing. The other attributes it to specific behaviors they changed deliberately. The first person has had a good experience. The second has built self-efficacy.

Albert Bandura's research on self-efficacy identified four sources from which people develop belief in their own capability. The most durable of these is mastery experience: a specific instance of successful performance that the person attributes to their own deliberate action. The attribution is not incidental to the mechanism. It is the mechanism. A successful call attributed to a good day produces nothing that compounds. The same call attributed to a specific behavior the participant changed on purpose produces a measurable and durable shift in how they assess their own capability the next time a similar situation arises.

Today's work is attribution training. Not reflection on how far you have come. Not a summary of the program. The specific behavior that produced the specific outcome — named, documented, owned.

You have four measurement points in this program: Day 01, Day 12, Day 22, and today. That trajectory is more informative than a before/after. It shows you where the conditioning accelerated, where it plateaued, and where Phase 4 integration produced what Phase 3 rehearsal had begun but not completed. Read the shape of that trajectory as data — not as a grade.

The scorecard is the primary exercise. The close rate delta review follows. The attribution exercise closes the day by building the five connections that Day 30 needs to produce a declaration that is evidence-based rather than aspirational.

One instruction before you start: bring your Day 12 workbook and your Day 28 evidence inventory. The participant who arrives at this scorecard with those documents has everything they need. The one who does not will have to reconstruct from memory — which is possible, but less precise.

The Science

Julian Rotter's locus of control research (1954, developed across subsequent decades) demonstrated that individuals who attribute outcomes to their own behavior — internal locus of control — perform more consistently under evaluative pressure than those who attribute outcomes to external factors: luck, market conditions, the quality of the lead. The performance gap is not explained by skill level. It is explained by attribution pattern.

Bandura's self-efficacy research extends this: when a mastery experience is attributed to a specific behavior the person changed deliberately, it produces a durable increase in the person's belief in their own capability in similar future situations. When the same experience is attributed to luck, the favorable outcome registers but the capability belief does not update. The close rate delta review today is not a numbers exercise. The attribution is the exercise. What specific behavioral change drove the difference in that outcome? That question, answered specifically and repeatedly, is what builds an internal attribution pattern. That pattern is what compounds over the next twelve months.

Improvement attributed to luck produces relief. Improvement attributed to specific behavior produces capability.
